How to Lower Your Car Insurance Premium: 12 Proven Strategies

By InsuranceTipsPro Editorial TeamJune 2, 20257 min read
IT
InsuranceTipsPro Editorial Team Last Updated: June 2025 • Reviewed for accuracy
This article is for educational purposes. Rates and coverage vary by state and insurer. Consult a licensed insurance professional for personalized advice.

Key Takeaways

  • Bundling auto and home insurance typically saves 10–25% on both policies.
  • Raising your deductible from $500 to $1,000 can cut your premium by 15–30%.
  • Good driver discounts can save 10–40% if you have a clean record for 3+ years.
  • Shopping quotes every 6–12 months is the single fastest way to find savings.
  • Usage-based programs can cut premiums up to 30% for low-mileage or careful drivers.

The average American pays over $1,700 per year for car insurance — but millions of drivers overpay simply because they never shop around or ask about discounts. The good news: reducing your premium doesn't mean cutting corners on protection. With the right strategies, you can save hundreds of dollars annually while keeping solid coverage.

Here are 12 proven strategies to lower your car insurance premium.

1. Shop Around Every Year

This is the single most powerful way to save. Insurance companies use different rating algorithms, so the same driver can get wildly different quotes from different insurers. A 2023 study found that drivers who shopped around saved an average of $461 per year.

Make it a habit to compare quotes at renewal time — or even mid-policy. You can switch insurers at any time without penalty. Use online comparison tools, contact independent agents who work with multiple carriers, or get quotes directly from insurer websites. Aim to compare at least three to five companies.

2. Bundle Your Policies

Most insurers offer a multi-policy discount — typically 5%–25% — when you combine auto with homeowners, renters, or life insurance. If you're currently insuring your home and car with different companies, you're almost certainly leaving money on the table.

Beyond the premium savings, bundling simplifies your life: one insurer, one bill, one renewal date. Just make sure bundling doesn't lock you into a bad deal on either policy — always compare the bundled price against shopping them separately.

3. Raise Your Deductible

Your deductible is the amount you pay out of pocket before insurance kicks in on a collision or comprehensive claim. Raising it from $500 to $1,000 can cut those coverage costs by 10%–40%.

The math only makes sense if you have the savings to cover the higher deductible in a pinch. A good rule of thumb: only raise your deductible to an amount you could genuinely afford to pay tomorrow without financial stress. If that's $1,000, great. If it's $500, stick there.

4. Ask About Every Discount

Insurers offer dozens of discounts, but many go unclaimed because drivers don't know to ask. Common discounts include:

  • Good driver discount — 5–10+ years without accidents or violations
  • Good student discount — full-time students with a B average or better
  • Defensive driving course — completing an approved course (often 5–10% off)
  • Low mileage discount — driving fewer than 7,500–10,000 miles per year
  • Vehicle safety features — airbags, anti-lock brakes, anti-theft systems
  • Loyalty discount — though sometimes shopping around beats loyalty
  • Occupation/affiliation discounts — educators, military, certain employers or alumni groups
  • Paperless/autopay discount — small but worth it

Call your insurer and specifically ask: "What discounts am I currently receiving, and what discounts might I qualify for?" You'll often be surprised.

5. Try Usage-Based or Pay-Per-Mile Insurance

Usage-based insurance (UBI) programs — like Progressive's Snapshot, State Farm's Drive Safe & Save, or Allstate's Drivewise — track your driving behavior via a mobile app or device. Safe driving habits (smooth braking, avoiding late-night driving, low mileage) can earn discounts of 10%–30%.

Pay-per-mile programs like Metromile charge a base rate plus a per-mile rate, making them ideal for drivers who work from home or drive fewer than 8,000 miles per year. If you drive 5,000 miles annually, you could cut your premium in half compared to a standard policy.

6. Improve Your Credit Score

In most states, insurers use a credit-based insurance score as a rating factor. Drivers with poor credit can pay 50%–100% more than those with excellent credit — even with identical driving records. (California, Hawaii, Massachusetts, and Michigan prohibit this practice.)

Improving your credit score takes time, but the payoff on insurance premiums can be significant. Paying bills on time, reducing credit card balances, and disputing errors on your credit report are the most effective steps. When your score improves noticeably, request a re-rating from your insurer or shop for a new quote.

7. Drop Unnecessary Coverage on Older Cars

Collision and comprehensive coverage make financial sense when your car is worth significantly more than the cost of the coverage. A general guideline: if your car is worth less than 10 times your annual collision/comprehensive premium, consider dropping those coverages. Our guide on how much car insurance you actually need explains when dropping coverage makes financial sense.

For example, if your car is worth $3,000 and collision costs $400/year with a $500 deductible, you'd only ever collect at most $2,500 on a total-loss claim — and you're paying $400 per year for that privilege. For older, fully-paid-off cars, liability-only coverage is often the smarter financial choice.

Check your car's current market value at Kelley Blue Book or Edmunds, then compare against your current premium plus deductible.

8. Pay Your Premium Annually

Most insurers charge installment fees — ranging from $5 to $15 per payment — when you pay monthly or quarterly. Paying your full annual premium upfront eliminates these fees and often earns a small discount (typically 2%–8%). If cash flow allows, this is a simple and guaranteed way to save.

More Quick Wins

9. Remove Young Drivers When They Leave Home

Teen and young adult drivers dramatically increase premiums. If your 22-year-old child moves out and has their own car and policy, remove them from your policy immediately. Keeping them listed when they no longer live in your household (and don't drive your cars) is both unnecessary and expensive.

10. Choose Your Next Car Wisely

Insurance rates vary significantly by vehicle. Sports cars, luxury vehicles, and cars with high theft rates or expensive repair costs cost more to insure. Before buying a new car, get insurance quotes on the specific models you're considering — the differences can be dramatic.

11. Maintain a Clean Driving Record

Accidents and violations typically increase your premium for three to five years. The best long-term strategy is simply avoiding them: obey speed limits, don't drive distracted, and consider telematics programs that reward safe driving. A single at-fault accident can increase your premium 30%–50%.

12. Review Your Coverage Annually

Life changes — you pay off a car loan (you can drop collision), your teen goes to college more than 100 miles away (often qualifies for a discount), or you start working from home (fewer miles = lower risk). Review your policy at renewal each year and update your profile to reflect your current situation.

Pro tip: Don't just compare price — compare coverage. A policy that's $200 cheaper but has lower liability limits, a higher deductible, or excludes important coverages isn't actually a better deal. Always compare policies at the same coverage levels.

How Much Can You Save?

Combining multiple strategies can produce dramatic savings. A driver who shops around (saves $200), bundles policies (saves $150), raises their deductible (saves $100), and enrolls in a telematics program (saves $120) could realistically cut their annual premium by $570 or more — with no reduction in meaningful protection.
